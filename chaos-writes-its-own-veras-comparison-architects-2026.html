Chaos graded six rendering tools. Its own tool won.

This week Chaos, the company that makes Veras, put up a post called "AI Architectural Rendering Tools in 2026: 6 Options Compared." Six tools went in: Veras, Midjourney, Rendair AI, Archsynth, xFigura, Artlist. One tool is Chaos's own product. That tool came out on top. Nobody rigged anything, and that is exactly what makes it worth reading closely.

Nora Sinclair, Vista Studios
7 min read · Jul 2026 · Analysis

Start with what the post actually says, because most of it checks out. Chaos scored six AI rendering tools across five categories: BIM and CAD integration, AI quality and creative control, processing speed, pricing and value, and learning curve. Veras is described as the only one of the six with direct integration into seven major BIM and CAD platforms. It runs on Google's Nano Banana 2 engine with a feature called Geometry Override, described as granular control over how the model interprets your model. Pricing starts at 29 dollars a month on the annual plan, with unlimited Stable Diffusion renders and a quota on the premium tier. None of that is a stretch. It is a fair description of the product.

The other five get real treatment too. Midjourney is called out as the most visually striking of the group, at 10 dollars a month, with no BIM integration at all. Rendair AI is the cheapest, around 7.60 dollars a month at the student rate, running entirely in a browser. Archsynth charges per render, about 0.049 dollars a pop. xFigura only works inside Rhino. Artlist is a general creative suite that was never built for architecture and it shows. Each of those is a real, checkable fact about a real product. This is not a hit piece about a company lying.

The scoring decided the winner before anyone opened a tool

Here is the part worth sitting with. The post states its own conclusion plainly: integration depth, not image quality alone, is the primary differentiator among professional tools. That is a defensible position. It is also the one category where Veras cannot lose, because Veras is a BIM plugin first and an image generator second. Midjourney was never going to compete on Revit integration. Neither was Artlist. Grading five general-purpose or single-platform tools against a category built around plugging into seven CAD programs is not a rigged test. It is a test where the answer was baked into the question.

Swap the lead category to image quality, the thing most architects actually judge a render on first, and Midjourney is the one the post itself calls the most visually striking. Swap it to price, and Rendair AI or Archsynth's per-render model wins outright for anyone doing occasional concept work rather than a daily production pipeline. The five categories were not arbitrary, but they were chosen by the company that owns one of the six answers, and the category that decided the outcome happens to be the one that company already dominates by design.

Where the other five actually win

Reading the post's own numbers against each other, three genuine wins show up outside Veras, and none of them get much room in the framing.

  • Price floor. Rendair AI at roughly 7.60 dollars a month is a quarter of Veras's annual rate, and Archsynth's per-render pricing means a studio doing a handful of concept images a month pays almost nothing. Veras's unlimited tier only pays for itself once you are rendering constantly.
  • Raw image quality. The post's own words put Midjourney ahead on visual strength. If the deliverable is a mood board, not a construction-stage plate held to your Revit geometry, that ranking flips the recommendation.
  • Simplicity. Rendair AI runs in a browser with no plugin, no host application, no license tied to a specific CAD seat. For a solo architect testing the water, that is a real advantage the integration-first scoring never gets to count.

What this is actually an example of

None of the facts in the Chaos post appear to be false. Geometry Override is a real feature. Seven platforms is a real number. 29 dollars is a real price. The dishonesty, if there is any, is not in the data, it is in the structure: a company that sells one of six products picked the axis the comparison would run on, and that axis happens to be the one it already wins by construction. This is not unique to Chaos. It is how nearly every vendor-published comparison works, on every industry's blog, and the reason it works is that readers grade the conclusion instead of the scoring rubric underneath it.

How to actually read a post like this

You do not need to throw the post out. You need to read it in a different order than it was written. Vendor comparisons are written verdict-first: pick the category your product wins, then build the scorecard backward from that category. Reading it usefully means undoing that order.

Start with the five categories themselves, not the scores inside them. Ask which category was placed first, because first is where a reader's attention lands hardest and where a post spends the most words justifying itself. In this case that is BIM and CAD integration, a category that exists almost entirely because Veras is good at it. Then ask which categories got the least space. Pricing and learning curve each got a sentence or two per tool here, while the integration section ran long with specifics: seven platforms named, Geometry Override explained, the Nano Banana 2 engine credited by name. Length is itself a signal about where the post wants your eye.

Then do the substitution test. Take the same six tools and re-rank them assuming a different studio need, one the post did not choose to lead with. A firm doing three concept renders a month for early client pitches has a different winner than a firm producing forty construction-stage plates a week tied to a live Revit model. The Chaos post is honest about the second use case and silent about the first, because the first is where Rendair AI's browser tool and Archsynth's per-render pricing start to look like the smarter buy.

Our take

We are not accusing Chaos of fabricating anything, and readers should not either. The specs check out. The problem is that a five-category scorecard written by a company selling one of the contestants will always find a reason that contestant wins, because the company picks which five categories exist. That is not a scandal, it is a structural fact about vendor content, and it applies to every "best tools compared" post published by a company that also sells a tool, on any subject, not just this one.

The fix is not to distrust the numbers. It is to separate the criteria from the winner before you read the verdict. Decide first what you actually need this quarter: full BIM integration because you render constantly, a cheap browser tool because you render twice a month, or raw visual punch because the deliverable is a pitch board nobody will hold against your model. Answer that, and the Chaos post becomes a useful spec sheet instead of a scoreboard. Skip that step and you have just let a vendor pick your winner for you, using real numbers, honestly reported, arranged to point one direction.
