Tool review · 17 May 2026 · 8 min read
Live residential project, six frames

MyArchitectAI, the tool every listicle keeps placing next to Veras.

MyArchitectAI surfaces on nearly every AI render comparison written this year. It rotates through the same slot every time, somewhere in the middle of the list, next to mnml.ai and ArchiVinci, two or three positions below Veras. What none of those write-ups do is explain what the tool actually feels like to use on a real project. We spent an afternoon running it through a live residential scheme, frame by frame, and got an answer that surprised us in both directions.

Nora Sinclair, Vista Studios
8 min read · May 2026 · Vista Studios tested · Live residential scheme

The pattern is familiar enough that we have stopped reading the lists. Some publication writes the seasonal "best AI rendering tools for architects" piece, the writer reaches for the same six tools, and MyArchitectAI lands in the middle of the table with a one-line caption nobody verifies. We have done this on our own pages too, until we noticed the obvious problem. There is no first-person practitioner write-up of MyArchitectAI anywhere on the architectural press. Plenty of marketing copy from the vendor. Plenty of placement on listicles. Nothing about what it does on a Tuesday afternoon when you have a deliverable in two days.

So we did the work. Six frames, the residential scheme we have been using for these reviews all month, the same references and prompt structure we ran through mnml.ai and ArchiVinci. Same coffee, same calendar block, same room. The results separate cleanly into two stories. One is about a tool that does one job better than any of its peers. The other is about a tool that disappoints in the place its marketing makes the loudest claim.

The tool the marketing describes, versus the tool you actually open

The MyArchitectAI homepage leads with photoreal interiors. The hero reel is wood floors and morning light, generously dressed living spaces, the kind of imagery that sells well on real-estate adjacent searches. The pricing tier copy talks about "interior visualization" and "AI staging" before it talks about exterior rendering. If you only read the marketing, you would walk in expecting an interior staging tool with exterior renders as a side product.

The tool you open behaves differently. The interface presents interior and exterior modes side by side, with the same upload-a-sketch or upload-a-photo entry path, the same prompt and style selectors, the same iteration buttons. There is no hint in the UI that one of these modes has been tuned harder than the other. You only find that out by feeding it work and watching what comes back.

Where the test started

We uploaded six images from the residential scheme. Three were SketchUp viewport exports of the exterior at concept stage, two were schematic interior sketches with rough furniture placement, and one was a photographic site reference of the existing context the new build was replacing. The prompts were short and specific. Materials called out, light condition called out, atmosphere left to the tool. No special tricks, no negative prompts, no inpainting passes. The kind of setup an architect would actually use during a working session, not the kind a Twitter thread would.

What MyArchitectAI does better than its peers

The interior frames came back convincing. More than convincing, by a margin we did not expect. The wood floor handling has a softness to the grain direction that mnml.ai routinely gets wrong. The light spill across a sectional sofa held a believable falloff, with shadows that respected the geometry of the room rather than smearing into the carpet. The styling was tasteful in a way that is hard to articulate but obvious side by side. The throws were folded the way an interior designer would fold them. The plant in the corner had a single, well-chosen species, not the AI default of a confused fern-monstera hybrid.

The schematic-to-photoreal conversion on the interiors did something neither mnml.ai nor ArchiVinci does well. It preserved the spatial intent of the sketch without flattening the proportions. The eyeline through the kitchen to the dining room held. The window placements stayed where we put them. Where the original sketch had been ambiguous about ceiling height, the tool guessed in a way that read as architectural, not generic. We have spent enough hours fighting tools that turn a five-meter living room into a four-meter living room to register when one does not.

The interiors are the best we have seen from a single-purpose AI tool this year. The exteriors are not in the same conversation.

Where the tool falls apart

The exterior frames were a different story. The first viewport, a corner view of the new build against its neighbour, came back with two roof planes that did not meet. The brick coursing on the gable changed scale halfway up the wall. The window mullions, which had been clean in the SketchUp source, multiplied themselves into thicker bands that aged the building by a decade. We ran the same view three more times with three different prompt variations, and the geometry failures repeated in different places each time. The renders were not unusable, exactly. They were the kind of thing you would never let leave the office.

The second exterior, a streetscape view, was better, in the sense that the building had a coherent shape. But the context buildings turned into pastiche. The neighbour's stone facade became a stucco facade. The existing tree, which the brief explicitly preserved, was replaced with a row of three identical maples. For a tool that bills itself as suitable for early-stage exterior presentations, the gap between "renders something" and "renders the actual project" is wide enough to make the use case suspect.

We tried the same exterior in Veras and in mnml.ai for comparison. Veras held the geometry cleanly, as it always does. mnml.ai mangled it differently, but the failures were stylistic rather than structural. MyArchitectAI's failure was the worst kind, the kind that produces a confident-looking image that has changed the design.

The honest read, where MyArchitectAI actually belongs

The marketing positions MyArchitectAI as a general AI render tool. The work tells a sharper story. It is an interior visualization tool with an exterior mode attached, and the two modes are not built to the same standard. That is not a failure of the product. It is a failure of how the product is marketed, and how the listicles repeat that marketing without checking it.

If you are an interior designer, or an architect on a residential project where the inside frames matter more than the outside frames, MyArchitectAI earns a slot in your stack ahead of mnml.ai for interior work. The styling, the light handling, the schematic-to-photoreal path are all genuinely better than what its closest peers produce. The pricing makes it easy to add as a second seat to whatever you are already running for exteriors.

If you are an architect whose work lives in massing and facade studies, the tool will let you down. Not catastrophically, not in a way that ruins a deliverable, but in a way that requires you to keep a second tool open for every exterior view. At that point you are not running MyArchitectAI as your render tool. You are running Veras as your render tool and MyArchitectAI as your interior staging tool, which is a perfectly reasonable stack and not the one the homepage describes.

Our take, when to reach for it

Reach for MyArchitectAI when the project is residential, the client is interior-led, and the renders that matter are the ones inside the building. The styling will do work for you. The light will hold. The schematic-to-photoreal path will turn a Tuesday sketch into a Friday presentation faster than mnml.ai or ArchiVinci will.

Skip MyArchitectAI when the project is commercial, when the renders that matter are exterior massing studies, or when the geometry of the design is the design. The tool will rearrange it. That is fine for staging a kitchen. It is not fine for showing a planning committee what the building looks like.

Run MyArchitectAI alongside Veras if your work spans both. Use Veras for the building and MyArchitectAI for the rooms inside it. The pricing makes the double seat easy, and the two tools complement each other better than either of them complements mnml.ai or ArchiVinci. It is the cleanest two-tool stack in the under-fifty-dollar tier of the market right now, once you accept what each tool is actually for.

Open a MyArchitectAI account this week and feed it the interior frames from your current residential project. Use the prompts you would use anywhere else, no special syntax. Then run the same frames through whatever you currently use for interiors. If MyArchitectAI does not win on at least four of six, we will read the next listicle that includes it with fresh eyes. We do not expect to.
