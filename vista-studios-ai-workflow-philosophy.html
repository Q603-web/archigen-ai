Why Vista Studios Chose AI-First Workflows in 2025

Vista Studios tested dozens of AI tools across two years of live projects. Here is what we kept, what we discarded, and why the shift from render-on-request to AI-first changed everything.

Vista Studios Editorial
9 min read · Published April 2026 · Based on two years of project work

In January 2024, our studio had three full-time visualization artists and a rendering queue that never emptied. By the end of that year, we had restructured the entire pipeline around AI-first workflows. The team shrank by one. The output doubled. More importantly, the kind of decisions we were making in the design phase started to change.

This piece is less a triumphant case study and more an account of what we tried, what we kept, and what we honestly regret spending time on. The space moves fast enough that we are writing this partly so we remember.

The Decision Point

The turning point came on a mixed-use project in late 2023. A developer client had asked for four facade treatments by Friday. We had a single production render for one of them, and a week's worth of work to produce the other three. Our lead artist mocked up a rough version of the second option in Midjourney over coffee on Tuesday morning. The client picked it in the Wednesday review and asked us to push it further.

Nothing about that render would have survived technical review. The brick coursing was fictional. The window proportions were slightly off. But the decision it triggered — the one that mattered, the one that set the direction of the scheme — was a good one. And it happened six days earlier than it would have without the tool.

That was the argument. Not that AI made our work faster to deliver, but that it moved decisions earlier in the process, where they are cheaper and more honest.

What We Tested Over 24 Months

The list is long and not all of it was useful. Between early 2024 and late 2025 we ran meaningful project work through Midjourney (v5 through v8.1), Stable Diffusion via Automatic1111, ComfyUI with SDXL and FLUX, Leonardo.ai, Krea, Chaos Veras, Rendair, Adobe Firefly, Luma Dream Machine, Runway Gen-3, Kling, Higgsfield, Topaz Gigapixel, Magnific, and a handful of things that either shut down or pivoted before we finished evaluating them.

We set a rule early on: a tool had to ship a deliverable on a real, paying project before it was allowed to enter the stack. No personal experiments counted. This cut evaluation time in half and made scoring obvious — either it survived the client call or it didn't.

What Stayed in the Stack

Midjourney stayed because nothing else matches it for atmospheric and material intuition. V8.1 is the current benchmark. We use it in the earliest phase, for mood boards, site-in-context studies, and any image where the point is to convey feeling rather than geometry.

ComfyUI stayed because it is the only tool that gives us reproducibility. When we need the same shot rendered eight ways with the same light, the same camera, and the same material palette, a ComfyUI workflow with ControlNet and IP-Adapter is the only thing that delivers. We have a production-grade upscale pipeline that runs on a rented A100 overnight.

Leonardo stayed as the finishing tool. Nothing we generate in Midjourney leaves the building at print resolution without passing through Leonardo's upscaler or inpainting. It is less famous than Magnific but better behaved at architectural scales.

Krea stayed, reluctantly, for real-time exploration in internal design review. We do not put it in front of clients yet. It is a sketch tool.

What Got Cut

Rendair produced images that were perfectly competent and completely forgettable. We found ourselves regenerating everything in Midjourney or Veras anyway.

Stable Diffusion via Automatic1111 got cut because ComfyUI's node-based workflow made it obsolete for us. The learning curve was identical, the ceiling was lower, and the community had moved on.

Runway we still use occasionally, but not in the core loop. For image-to-video handoff we have moved to Kling for realism and Higgsfield for drone-style motion. Runway's Gen-3 is good. The others are better for architecture.

Luma Dream Machine was impressive in 2024 and has not kept pace. We tested its recent releases and the output no longer justifies the credits.

There is also a small pile of architecture-specific SaaS tools that marketed hard in 2024 and quietly pivoted to interior design or real estate. We are not naming them. The point is that betting on a specialized wrapper over a frontier model is usually a losing trade. The base model catches up, and the wrapper has to find a new reason to exist.

The Workflow That Actually Stuck

Our current pipeline is four stages. Every project goes through some version of it, with the last two stages skipped for schematic-only work.

Stage one — concept and mood. Midjourney for fast generation of context, atmosphere, and facade character. Output lives on a shared Are.na board. This stage is where the design director spends most of their AI time.

Stage two — geometry anchor. A rough SketchUp or Rhino massing gets exported to depth map and fed into a ComfyUI workflow with ControlNet-Depth. This is where the image starts to look like our actual building, not a nearby cousin of it.

Stage three — consistent views. Chaos Veras runs on the fleshed-out Rhino model when we need three to eight variations of the same shot without breaking continuity. This is the only part of the workflow where accuracy is non-negotiable.

Stage four — finish. Upscale and detail pass in Leonardo. Final post-production in Photoshop with Firefly generative fill for sky replacement, entourage cleanup, and anything the model got wrong around edges.

The pipeline is less impressive than the fact that we know where each image goes and why. That is the part that took two years.

What AI Rendering Still Cannot Do

It cannot draft a construction detail. It cannot produce a planning-accurate axonometric. It cannot hold material consistency across a set of documentation drawings without very specific scaffolding that is, for now, more work than just rendering the drawings conventionally.

It does not handle reflective or transparent surfaces at the level of a trained V-Ray operator. Glazing in particular remains a weakness across every model we tested. We almost always composite glass reflections manually in the finishing stage.

And it cannot replace the moment where the project architect sits down with the principal and decides, from the drawings, what the building wants to be. AI renders accelerate that moment. They do not substitute for it.

Is It Worth It for a Small Firm?

For a practice of three to fifteen people: yes, unambiguously, but only if one person is willing to own the pipeline. Treating AI tools as a novelty that anyone can pick up on a slow afternoon produces exactly the muddled, hallucinated output that gives the whole category a bad reputation.

Make it somebody's job. Write down the workflow. Version-control the prompts. The studios we know that are doing this well have all landed on some version of the same answer — an internal document that describes how the practice prompts, renders, and finishes. It reads like a house style guide from a graphic design studio circa 2005.

The productivity gain is real but it is not the reason to do this. The reason to do this is that the conversations you have with clients in the schematic phase become richer, earlier, and more honest about what the building will feel like. That is worth the pipeline.

Vista Studios is an architecture practice working across residential, cultural, and adaptive reuse projects. ArchiGen AI is our publication on tools and methods.
